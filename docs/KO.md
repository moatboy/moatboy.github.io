# Coca-Cola

{: .label .label-purple }

Management: 3/5

{: .label .label-green }

Catalyst: 2/5

Pessimistic value: $270 B

Coca-Cola's moat stems primarily from its **brand** and **distribution network**.  These intangible assets, as discussed in Pat Dorsey's *The Little Book That Builds Wealth*, create a mini-monopoly, allowing the company to extract a lot of value from its customers.  Like Tiffany's is able to charge a premium for its diamonds, Coke commands a higher price than generic sodas. Furthermore, Coke benefits from demand-side economies of scale as described by Dorsey in *The Little Book That Builds Wealth*: the more restaurants that sell Coke products, the more valuable it becomes to consumers, and vice versa. The bottling partners operate with a franchise model as described in McKinsey's *Valuation*, which also enhances its value. As discussed in Counterpoint Global's *Measuring the Moat*, the firm’s global bottling system has contributed to its success and sustainable value creation. These advantages, combined with smart strategic choices, have enabled it to maintain above-average returns on invested capital (ROIC) for a long time—a key characteristic of a wide-moat company. I am rating the moat as 4/5 because, while substantial, it's not unassailable.  Brands can lose their luster, patents can be challenged, and licensed can be revoked. Private-label products have also eroded some of the brand's pricing power.

I am rating Coca-Cola's management 3/5.  While the company has a long and mostly successful history, I remain skeptical about management's ability to allocate capital efficiently.  Like many mature businesses, as Aswath Damodaran explains in his *The Little Book of Valuation*, Coke increasingly turns to acquisitions for growth, and the success of such a strategy is dubious. In addition, like many mature companies, Coke maintains a very high payout ratio. This reflects management's pessimism about the company's organic growth, but it also leaves little room for reinvestment. As mentioned in *The Essays of Warren Buffet*, acquisitions create less shareholder value than organic growth. As discussed in *Valuation: Measuring and Managing the Value of Companies*, acquisitions are driven by ego as much as the hope of value creation.

* **Capital Allocation:**  As the McKinsey valuation book emphasizes, acquisitions are often motivated more by empire building than profitability.  Coke's management seems to recognize the difficulty of substantially boosting organic growth.  This is evident in their reliance on stock buybacks and acquisitions—both of which, as highlighted in *The Essays of Warren Buffet*, have a mixed record in terms of creating shareholder value.  While share buybacks may temporarily pump up earnings per share, they do nothing to increase the underlying value of the business.  Acquisitions often come with an expensive price tag and the risk of overpaying.
* **Communication:**  As discussed in McKinsey's *Valuation*, management seems mostly forthcoming in terms of its communications with financial markets, which is a positive sign.  However, it's hard to gauge how well management conveys its long-term view to intrinsic investors.
* **Compensation:** Executive compensation packages are in line with industry peers, according to their latest proxy statement which is not necessarily problematic.
* **Insider Ownership:** According to the latest proxy filings, insider ownership is very low which could be interpreted as a sign that management is not fully aligned with shareholder interests.


**Business Overview**

* **Revenues Distribution:**  Coca-Cola operates through a global distribution network, with revenues coming from various geographic regions, developed and developing markets.  The bottling investment group, as discussed in Counterpoint Global's *Measuring the Moat*, is critical to Coca-Cola's strategy. The most significant part of the distribution network is managed by independent bottlers operating under a franchise model.


* **Industry Trends:**  Aswath Damodaran's *The Dark Side of Valuation* explains how changing consumer preferences have also added complexity to the industry landscape. The beverage industry is highly competitive and subject to changing consumer tastes.  In recent years, health concerns, particularly related to sugary drinks, have led to declining sales volumes for some of Coke's core brands.  In addition, private-label products continue to offer consumers a cheaper, albeit often inferior, alternative to well-known brands.  The industry has seen a number of companies invest heavily in healthier, lower-sugar alternatives, and management needs to continue to adapt to maintain a competitive advantage. 

* **Margins:**  The company's operating margins have been under pressure due to competition and increasing costs. As discussed in *The Essays of Warren Buffet*, increased costs can cut into margins, even as revenues grow. 
* **Competitive Landscape:** Coca-Cola competes against a slew of other beverage companies, large and small, including PepsiCo, Keurig Dr Pepper, and other smaller beverage producers around the globe. As highlighted in Counterpoint Global's *Measuring the Moat*, competition is intense within the industry.


* **What Makes the Company Different:**  Coca-Cola's competitive advantage stems from a combination of its strong brand name, global distribution network, and savvy marketing, although it is worth noting that all of these advantages have been threatened over the years. Their brand, as discussed in Dorsey's *The Little Book that Builds Wealth*, gives them pricing power, and their distribution network enhances the value of their product. It's a virtuous cycle.


**Financials In-Depth**

Coca-Cola's financial statements show a mature company with substantial free cash flow, but with limited organic growth potential.  

* **Cash Flows:** The company generated solid operating income but has invested heavily in share buybacks, which, as pointed out in *The Essays of Warren Buffet*, have done nothing to improve operating performance. It's worth noting that in the Q4 2023 earnings call, management expressed confidence in the strength of their cash flow but also noted that some of their cash flows come from buybacks that reduce cash available to fund growth or pay dividends. I decided to investigate the cash flow numbers further by examining the company's most recent 10-k. As shown in note 11 from Coca-Cola's 2023 10-k, in 2023 Coca-Cola had operating cash flow of $10.3 billion and spent $2.2 billion in capex. After paying dividends, buybacks are the only other significant use of Coca-Cola's cash flow, with $3.1 billion and $5.3 billion in share buybacks in 2022 and 2023, respectively. As discussed in *The Essays of Warren Buffet*, share buybacks have questionable benefit in the long term.
* **Growth:**  Revenue growth has been sluggish in recent years, although in the Q4 2023 earnings call, management noted that the firm had seen improved revenue growth in emerging markets.
* **Return on Capital:**  The company's return on invested capital has declined slightly in recent years.
* **Debt:**  The company's debt-to-equity ratio is relatively low, giving it some financial flexibility.



**Valuation**

To value Coca-Cola, I used a discounted cash flow (DCF) model—specifically the dividend discount model because it is difficult to estimate capex with intangible assets.  I discounted the expected dividends at the cost of equity, using the information on how to estimate FCFE from note 11 in Coca-Cola's 2023 10-k.

**Catalysts**

* **Emerging Market Growth:** As highlighted in the Q4 2023 earnings call, Coca-Cola has seen strong organic revenue growth in developing and emerging markets.  If this trend continues, it could provide a catalyst for the stock price.
* **New Product Innovation:**  Coca-Cola has been innovating with new products and flavors.  A successful new product launch could boost sales and potentially widen the company's moat.  
* **Cost-Cutting Initiatives:**  The company has been implementing cost-cutting measures.  If these initiatives are successful, they could boost margins and earnings.
* **Dividend Growth:** As shown in note 11 in Coca-Cola's 2023 10-k, since 1963, dividends have grown by at least 5% with dividends exceeding earnings in 2022 and 2023. As discussed in Graham's *The Intelligent Investor*, a continued emphasis on returning cash to shareholders through steadily rising dividends could make the stock more attractive.


{: .note }

I am rating the catalyst strength as 2/5, as these potential catalysts are fairly predictable and face significant uncertainty.
