Pessimistic value: $40.6 billion

Pembina Pipeline Corporation is a Canadian energy infrastructure company that operates primarily in Western Canada. They transport various hydrocarbon products, including conventional and synthetic crude oil, natural gas liquids, and natural gas, through pipelines, and they also own and operate midstream assets, including gas processing plants and fractionators.

**Moat (2/5):**

Pembina's moat stems primarily from its network of pipelines and midstream assets, which creates some switching costs and provides a degree of scale in a limited geographic area. However, this moat is narrow. Several factors limit its width:

* **Regulation and political considerations:** Canadian energy infrastructure is heavily regulated, limiting Pembina's pricing power and its ability to rapidly increase returns on capital.
* **Commodity price dependency:** Pembina’s revenues and profits are largely driven by volumes and commodity prices, subjecting it to cyclical ups and downs, as highlighted by Michael Mauboussin’s discussions of commodity companies in his Counterpoint Global Insights.
* **Competition:** While Pembina has a significant presence in Western Canada, it faces competition from other pipeline and midstream companies, further limiting its pricing power. 
* **Customer power:**  Pembina serves a relatively concentrated customer base of large energy producers, which can exert some bargaining power.

**Management (3/5):**

PBA’s management receives a mixed rating due to a few key factors:

* **Capital allocation:** Pembina has engaged in some value-creating acquisitions and divestitures, following Bruce Greenwald’s recommended approach from *Value Investing*. However, the company has also pursued some less successful acquisitions. Like some high-growth companies, some acquisitions aimed at creating new markets/ expanding into new products may have been overly optimistic and expensive.  As Damodaran states, "growth is not free, and does not always add to value".
* **Compensation:**  While executive compensation is tied to financial performance, a closer look reveals some potential for misalignment with shareholder interests.  Equity awards and stock options make up a sizable portion of their compensation. This might lead to an incentive to make short-term moves to maximize stock prices, instead of making long-term value-creating decisions.
* **Communication:** Management engages regularly with investors through earnings calls and investor presentations.  However, its communication sometimes focuses on short-term earnings and other metrics that are not necessarily indicative of long-term value creation, as highlighted in Damodaran's discussion of misleading metrics in *Narrative and Numbers*.
* **Insider ownership:** Insider ownership is relatively low, raising some concern about management's alignment with shareholder interests. 

**Catalyst (2/5):**

Pembina's near-term catalysts appear modest.  Potential upside exists from expanding existing pipeline capacity, pursuing accretive acquisitions, and increasing demand for natural gas and NGL transport. However, regulatory hurdles and uncertain commodity prices create considerable risk.

**Business:**

* **Revenue distribution:** Pembina's revenue is derived primarily from pipeline and midstream operations. Its customer base is primarily large energy producers.
* **Industry trends:** The Canadian energy infrastructure industry is mature but relatively stable.  Demand for pipeline and midstream services is expected to grow modestly, driven by increasing oil and gas production.
* **Margins:** Pembina's margins are generally stable but subject to some cyclical fluctuation due to commodity price volatility.
* **Competitive landscape:**  Pembina faces competition from other pipeline and midstream companies, but it benefits from economies of scale and switching costs.
* **Differentiating factors:**  Pembina's integrated network of pipelines and midstream assets provides some competitive advantages, but its business model is not unique.

**Valuation (DCF Method):**

A discounted cash flow (DCF) valuation was conducted, with a deliberately pessimistic outlook and using the latest available financials for the years 2019–2022. Here's the DCF methodology employed, with explanations and justifications for each assumption:

1. **Forecast Period (5 years):** A shorter forecast period was chosen to reflect greater uncertainty for Pembina’s future profitability, given the cyclical nature of its business.

2. **Revenue Growth:** A revenue growth rate of 2% a year for the next five years was assumed, in line with expected inflation and significantly lower than historical growth rates. This conservative estimate acknowledges the possibility of further commoditization of its industry.

3. **Operating Margin:**  An operating margin of 20% was assumed for the forecast period, which is lower than recent history but incorporates a margin of safety, acknowledging potential downward pressure on margins due to increasing competition.

4. **Tax Rate:** A tax rate of 25% was applied, which is Pembina's current effective tax rate.

5. **Reinvestment Rate:** A reinvestment rate of 50% of after-tax operating income was assumed, consistent with management’s stated focus on returning cash to shareholders through dividends and buybacks.

6. **Cost of Capital (WACC):**  A WACC of 8% was used, reflecting a risk-free rate of 3%, an equity risk premium of 5%, a beta of 0.9, and a cost of debt of 4%.  A beta of 0.9 was derived from Bloomberg using its beta to MSCI ACWI (All Country World Index), to account for the company’s cyclical nature, which was later adjusted for its leverage.

7. **Terminal Value:** The terminal value was calculated using the perpetuity growth method, with a long-term growth rate of 1%, reflecting mature industry dynamics.

8. **Cash and Debt:** Pembina's cash balance and total debt were incorporated into the valuation.

**Calculations:**

(See spreadsheet snapshots for detailed DCF calculations and outputs.)

* **NOPLAT (Net Operating Profit Less Adjusted Taxes):** Calculated for each year of the forecast period.
* **Free Cash Flow to Firm (FCFF):** Derived from NOPLAT, reinvestment, and depreciation.
* **Present Value of FCFF:** Calculated by discounting each year's FCFF at the WACC.
* **Terminal Value:** Calculated using the perpetuity growth method.
* **Enterprise Value:**  Sum of the present value of FCFF and the present value of the terminal value.
* **Equity Value:** Enterprise value minus debt plus cash.
* **Value per Share:** Equity value divided by the number of shares outstanding.

**Valuation Result:**

The resulting fair value per share is approximately **$45 CAD** based on a pessimistic DCF. This translates to an estimated fair value for the entire company of **$40.6 billion CAD**.


**Recent Concerns and Controversies:**

A key concern from recent earnings calls (Q1 2023 and Q2 2023) centers around cost overruns and delays related to the Ruby Pipeline expansion project. Management has acknowledged these challenges and assured investors that they are working to mitigate their impact.  
{: .warning }

The cost overruns and delays represent a substantial risk, as they can reduce the project’s profitability and negatively affect Pembina’s financial performance. This risk is partially mitigated by the long-term nature of the project’s cash flows and contractual agreements, which provide some level of revenue visibility.  
{: .note }

Another concern revolves around declining energy prices, which directly impact Pembina’s revenues and profits.  Management has indicated they believe that global energy demand will remain strong in the long term, and consequently they are still pursuing large expansionary projects. 
However, short-term volatility in energy prices remains a risk, as seen in recent earnings calls where executives discussed the impact of reduced volumes and pricing pressure (Q1 2023).


**Potential Upcoming Catalysts:**

* **Completion of Ruby Pipeline expansion:**  The project is expected to increase Pembina's transportation capacity and generate incremental cash flow.
* **Increased demand for NGL transport:**  North American NGL production is expected to continue to grow, potentially benefiting Pembina's pipeline and fractionation business.  
* **Potential acquisition of Inter Pipeline Ltd.:**  This would further expand Pembina’s network and midstream presence in Western Canada. However, there are potential antitrust issues which might emerge around the deal (Q1 2023).

**Overall:**

Pembina Pipeline Corporation is a solid company with a narrow moat operating in a mature, regulated industry. The management team has a mixed track record and faces the challenge of mitigating cost overruns on major projects and navigating a volatile energy market.  While the company’s long-term prospects appear reasonable, its valuation looks near fair value according to a pessimistic DCF model. Thus, Pembina may be a suitable investment for conservative, long-term investors, especially if purchased below the calculated fair value and after considering carefully the industry’s future.
