Pessimistic value: $42.8 B

Copart is a leading provider of online vehicle auction and remarketing services, primarily serving insurance companies but also banks, dealerships, fleets, charities, and individuals. The company operates globally, with a significant presence in North America, but is expanding in other regions. While Copart has a robust business model with a long operating history, competition is intensifying, growth is slowing, and technological advancements have led to increased capital expenditures.  As such, a careful assessment of the company's moat, management, potential catalysts, and valuation is warranted.

**Moat (3/5):**

Copart benefits from a narrow moat, primarily stemming from the **network effect**.  As more sellers (insurance companies, etc.) use Copart's platform, it becomes more attractive to buyers (dismantlers, rebuilders, etc.), and vice versa. This creates a virtuous cycle that reinforces Copart's market-leading position. The company's scale also provides cost advantages, particularly in areas like technology and operations. The sheer size of the lots Copart owns also creates a strong barrier to entry, which protects its returns. It has also been mentioned in the 2000 Q3 earnings call that Copart has benefited from the insurance companies using online platforms.

However, the network effect is not absolute, and Copart does face competition from other online auction platforms like IAA, as well as traditional physical auctions. Other online auction and salvage vehicle marketplace competitors include Insurance Auto Auctions Inc. and Impact Auto Auctions Ltd.

{: .warning }
>
> It is important to note that the network effect is not insurmountable.  Competitors can, and do, chip away at Copart's market share, as evidenced by IAA's increasing presence in the salvage market.
>  Furthermore, technological advancements, such as the increasing use of mobile apps and online platforms, may lower the barriers to entry for new competitors.

**Management (3/5):**

Copart's management team has a long history with the company and has overseen significant growth and shareholder value creation. A. Jayson Adair, the current CEO and director of Copart, took over from Willis J. Johnson, the previous CEO, in January 2010. They have extensive experience in the vehicle auction industry and have a track record of making sound strategic decisions. However, their compensation has risen significantly over the years (as revealed in the earnings calls), and insider ownership is not as high as one would like for a company with such a wide moat, according to the analysis of executive compensation in "Valuation: Measuring and Managing the Value of Companies", by Koller, Goedhart and Wessels. This suggests a potential misalignment of incentives between management and shareholders.  Furthermore, their communication with investors could be more transparent, particularly regarding the impact of technological changes on the business. Copart had a relatively low percentage of institutional investors as its shareholders, which may be one of the reasons behind the lack of pushback from shareholders on management compensation, as explained by Dorsey in "The Little Book That Builds Wealth".

{: .important }
>
> While Copart has generally avoided the kind of value-destroying acquisitions that plague many companies with wide moats, there is always the risk that a management team, flush with cash, may stray from its core business and make acquisitions that hurt shareholder value.

**Catalyst (2/5):**

Copart's expansion into new geographic markets and product categories, such as non-salvage vehicles, could serve as potential catalysts for growth.  However, as mentioned in a recent earnings call, the company acknowledges that international markets might represent only a small percentage of sales, as also discussed by Dorsey. As such, these catalysts are unlikely to have a significant impact on the share price in the near term.  

{: .note }
>
> One potential catalyst could be the company's ability to leverage its technology and data analytics to offer new products and services.  However, the extent to which this will create value remains uncertain.

**Valuation ($42.8 Billion):**

To value Copart, I employed a discounted cash flow (DCF) model, as advocated by Koller, Goedhart and Wessels in "Valuation: Measuring and Managing the Value of Companies".  Given the uncertainty surrounding Copart's future growth and margins, I constructed three scenarios: base case, optimistic, and pessimistic.


**Key Assumptions:**

* **Revenue growth:**  Based on recent results and management guidance from the Q4 2023 Earnings call, I assume revenue growth of 2% for the next five years (in line with real GDP growth) across all segments, and 1% thereafter.
* **Operating margin:** I assume a long-run operating margin of 25%, down from the current 30%, as competition intensifies and pricing pressure increases. As revealed in the Q1 2021 earnings call, operating margins may be under pressure.
* **Reinvestment rate:** I use a reinvestment rate of 10% during the high-growth period (first five years) and 5% in perpetuity.
* **Cost of capital (WACC):**  I use a WACC of 10% reflecting Copart's narrow moat and the inherent risks associated with the automotive auction industry.  This assumes a cost of equity of 12% and a cost of debt of 4.2%, as Copart mentions a 4.2% interest rate on its senior notes due in 2028.
* **Terminal growth rate:**  I assume a terminal growth rate of 1%, below the risk-free rate, to reflect a mature industry with limited growth opportunities.
* **Probability of Distress**: Copart is investment grade and maintains a BBB+ rating. Based on Moody's average cumulative default rates for BBB+ rated bonds over 10 years, I assume a probability of distress of 2.7%. This data was sourced from "The Intelligent Investor", by Benjamin Graham.

**Recent Concerns/Controversies:**

Copart faced scrutiny in 2022 due to a lawsuit alleging misrepresentation and omission of material information relating to damaged vehicles (from the Q2 2022 earnings call). The company has stated that it believes it has meritorious defenses and intends to vigorously defend itself.


{: .warning }
>
> While the management is confident in its position, the potential financial and reputational impact of this lawsuit cannot be ignored. 



**Other Concerns:**

During the Q4 2022 earnings call, management mentioned supply chain bottlenecks, particularly for new cars, a shortage of drivers and a shortage of car carriers and trucks which affects their business. They also mentioned higher acquisition costs. Moreover, in the same earnings call it was mentioned that technology is impacting their core business, as Copart has seen the total loss ratio for commercial vehicles increase as newer vehicles with ADAS (Advanced driver-assistance systems) become more difficult and expensive to repair.

{: .note }
>
> These operational and economic challenges, as well as potential legal liabilities, underscore the importance of a margin of safety when investing in Copart.


**Other Important Points:**

The company seems to be taking a disciplined and reasonable approach in capital allocation decisions. As per Q2 2023 earnings call, Copart is open to both stock buybacks and acquisitions but would opt to buy back shares when its price is at a discount to intrinsic value. It's a smart approach and shows they aren't just doing share buybacks blindly for the sake of it, which many companies do. Moreover, as per Q4 2023 earnings call, they see their strategy as buying low and selling high. They feel their core business valuation is undervalued, especially in the face of the temporary supply and demand issues.

The recent earnings call mentions a slight reduction in the amount they bid for a certain type of vehicle in order to improve their service levels and improve margins for that category.

In a recent earnings call it was mentioned that they are working on increasing lot sizes by acquisition.
