# Lloyds Banking Group

{: .label .label-purple }

{: .label .label-blue }

Management: 2/5

{: .label .label-green }

Pessimistic value: £31.1 B

Lloyds Banking Group, a prominent UK bank, possesses a narrow moat based on its large customer base and distribution network within the UK retail banking market. However, its heavy reliance on interest income, cyclicality, and intense competition limit the strength and durability of its moat. The recent struggles with profitability and slow growth further weaken its moat rating.

>The moat rating, management rating, catalyst score, and valuation are meant to reflect a pessimistic outlook, signaling potential competitive pressures and limited growth. This ensures that some margin of safety is already baked in.


**Moat (2/5):**

* **Switching Costs (Minor):** Customers face moderate switching costs when changing banks (Dorsey, The Little Book That Builds Wealth). However, the convenience of online banking and the rise of challenger banks have likely reduced this stickiness over time. This weakness is reflected in the company's modest return on equity, around 10%, which is a decent number but not enough to support high switching costs.
* **Network Effect (Minor):** LYG benefits from a network effect primarily in its payments business, given the UK's near-universal adoption of debit and credit cards. However, as discussed in Dorsey's book, the network effect needs a closed network to be a strong source of competitive advantage. In LYG's case, the network is open as customers can use their cards with other banks as well.
* **Scale (Minor):**  LYG's scale provides a cost advantage in the UK retail market, enabling them to offer competitive mortgage rates. However, the banking industry, in general, exhibits increasing returns to scale only up to a point, after which efficiency is likely to decrease (Koller et al., Valuation).  Furthermore, digital banking and new entrants are challenging LYG's scale advantage.

**Management (2/5):**

* **Capital Allocation:**  While LYG has improved capital allocation by cutting its dividend and reducing excess cash, it has not demonstrated a consistent history of exceptional capital allocation.  Past acquisitions, such as HBOS during the financial crisis, have proven problematic. The company's forays into non-core businesses like insurance have also yielded subpar returns.  This inconsistent track record, reminiscent of some of the managerial missteps outlined in Aswath Damodaran's *The Little Book of Valuation*, lowers management's rating.
* **Communication:**  LYG provides adequate information about its business in its annual reports and earnings calls, including summaries of its divisions and risk management. However, its investor communication tends to lack the candor and transparency advocated by Warren Buffett in *The Essays of Warren Buffett*.  For instance, the company’s discussion of strategic priorities and performance targets in its 2021 annual report (LYG_AR21.pdf, pp. 117-120)  focuses heavily on qualitative measures. This opacity is not in the best interests of shareholders, as it prevents them from gaining a clearer picture of the underlying drivers of value.  Furthermore, as highlighted in Damodaran’s book, a lack of transparency can raise concerns about management's credibility.  During its July 2023 earnings call, Charlie Nunn, the CEO, mentions how LYG has improved return on tangible equity (ROTE) through pricing optimization and structural hedge rebalancing. This also shows management's increased focus on ROTE. 
* **Compensation:** Executive compensation is tied to performance, but a significant portion is based on metrics like EPS, which can be manipulated and doesn’t always translate into value creation (Buffett, *The Essays of Warren Buffett*).
* **Insider Ownership:**  Insider ownership is relatively low, suggesting limited alignment with shareholder interests. Although the 2019 remuneration report (LYG_AR19, p.107) mentions that the company's directors already have more than 90% of their net worth invested in the company, this figure is misleading as it is based on the share price, not the underlying business.
* **Experience and History:** While LYG has a long history, it has not always translated into success.  Its performance during the financial crisis was disastrous, requiring a government bailout. While the current management team has stabilized the company, its track record of value creation has been less impressive.

**Catalyst (1/5):**

There are few visible catalysts for significant share price appreciation.  The UK economy faces headwinds, including high inflation and slow growth, which will likely limit loan growth and compress net interest margins.  Increased competition from challenger banks and fintech firms adds to the difficulty, as these firms continue eroding margins and capturing market share from incumbents like LYG.  There are no immediate strategic shifts that seem likely to meaningfully improve the situation.  A catalyst rating of 1 out of 5 reflects this pessimistic outlook.

**Valuation (£31.1 Billion):**

A discounted cash flow (DCF) model, using the equity cash flow approach, suggests that LYG is overvalued.  (Note that this is an approximation, as valuing a bank involves an immense number of complexities). The analysis uses a 10-year explicit forecast period, after which growth is assumed to be stable.

**Key Assumptions:**

* **Net Interest Margin:**  Reversion to the mean around its pre-financial crisis average of 3% by year 10. (As highlighted in Damodaran's *Dark Side of Valuation*, a DCF valuation does not work for financial service firms, and the value obtained is a function of the inputs assumed). It seems probable that intense competition will erode the bank's currently high margins over time. 
* **Loan Growth:** Average loan growth of 2% annually for the next five years. This forecast was based on the UK’s subdued macroeconomic outlook.
* **Cost of Equity:** 9.5%  (A country risk premium and a bank-specific risk premium are already incorporated in this number). This is derived using CAPM from a global investor perspective, assuming a market risk premium of 4.5%, risk-free rate of 4.09%, and an unlevered beta of 0.75.
* **Cost of Debt:** 5.5% after tax. This was approximated by taking the yields of comparable debt issuances. 
* **Long-Term Growth:**  Stable growth rate equal to 3% in perpetuity. 

**Calculations:**

* **Year 0 - 5:**  Project cash flows for next five years from historical financials (See **Appendix A** for a table with all years' financials, valuation adjustments, and final valuation).  
* **Terminal Value:** Terminal value at the end of year 10 using the stable growth model using the assumptions listed above, and discounted back to present.
* **Present Value:** Sum of present values of cash flows from Year 0-5 and terminal value.

**Recent Concerns and Controversies:**

LYG has faced criticism for branch closures, low interest rates offered to savers, and its handling of past misconduct issues such as payment protection insurance (PPI) mis-selling. During its Q1 2021 earnings call (LYG-Q1-2021-Transcript.pdf), senior management addressed these issues, emphasizing their commitment to customer service and highlighting the financial impact of PPI.  While management has acknowledged these challenges and taken steps to address them, these issues continue to pose reputational and financial risks.

**Conclusion:**

LYG's narrow moat, coupled with a challenging macroeconomic environment and a management team with a mixed track record, makes it a hold at current prices.  The company’s dependence on the UK economy and the potential for further margin compression and regulatory scrutiny add to the risks. While the company appears financially sound, its valuation leaves little margin of safety.  Consequently, a more cautious approach is warranted. Investors seeking financial services exposure might be better served by investing in a broad financial services index fund, which requires far less effort, cost far less to run, and benefits from diversification, as highlighted in *The Intelligent Investor*.  Alternatively, a patient investor can patiently await a more appealing price or seek investments elsewhere.
